## 1.1 Background of the Study

India's civil services examination, conducted by the Union Public Service Commission (UPSC), is widely regarded as one of the most competitive and intellectually demanding examinations in the world. Every year, over **10 lakh (1 million) aspirants** register for the Civil Services Preliminary Examination, yet only a fraction — approximately **0.1% to 0.2%** — ultimately succeed in securing a position in the Indian Administrative Service (IAS), Indian Police Service (IPS), and other allied services. This extreme level of competition has given rise to a vast and rapidly expanding ecosystem of dedicated UPSC coaching institutes across India.

Cities such as Delhi (Old Rajinder Nagar), Hyderabad, Bengaluru, and Jaipur have emerged as prominent hubs for UPSC preparation, hosting hundreds of coaching institutes that collectively serve lakhs of aspirants annually. The Indian private coaching industry, of which UPSC preparation forms a significant segment, is estimated to be worth over **₹58,000 crore** and growing at a CAGR of approximately 9–12%, according to industry estimates. Institutes such as Insights IAS, IAS Baba, Vision IAS, Vajiram and Ravi, Forum IAS, and La Excellence have established themselves as large-scale, multi-batch operations with structured programs, dedicated faculty teams, and extensive study material ecosystems.

The growth of UPSC coaching institutes is not merely a commercial phenomenon — it reflects a deeper socio-economic reality of India. For millions of young aspirants from tier-2 and tier-3 cities, small towns, and rural backgrounds, these institutes represent the **primary gateway** to structured UPSC preparation. Many aspirants relocate to coaching hubs, often at significant personal and financial cost, investing anywhere from **Rs. 80,000 to Rs. 2,50,000** per year in coaching fees alone, in addition to accommodation and living expenses. The decision to enroll in a coaching institute is therefore not taken lightly — it is a high-stakes investment of time, money, and opportunity.

Given this context, the **quality and completeness of course delivery** carries enormous weight. When a course is delayed, poorly paced, or left incomplete due to operational mismanagement, the consequences extend far beyond administrative inconvenience. Students may miss critical portions of the UPSC syllabus, lose momentum in their preparation, and suffer psychological setbacks that affect their overall performance. In a competitive exam where **even a single mark can determine selection or rejection**, curriculum incompleteness caused by institutional operational failures is a serious concern.

Yet, despite the scale, stakes, and societal importance of this sector, UPSC coaching institutes have received **remarkably limited academic attention** from an operations management or service management perspective. Most existing research either focuses on the sociological dimensions of coaching culture (Desai, 2018; Nambissan and Ball, 2010) or on student success rates relative to coaching (Madan and Tewari, 2014). The **operational mechanics** of how these institutes are run — how batches are sized, how faculty are deployed, how workloads are distributed, and how these decisions affect the quality of course delivery — remain largely unexplored in the academic literature. This study seeks to fill that gap.

## 1.2 The Multi-Batch Operational Challenge

A defining and structurally unique characteristic of established UPSC coaching institutes is their **multi-batch operational model**. Unlike traditional educational institutions — schools, colleges, or universities — that operate on a single, unified academic calendar with one cohort per course per year, UPSC coaching institutes run **multiple overlapping batches simultaneously and continuously throughout the year**. Each batch represents a distinct cohort of students who enrolled at a different point in time, following the same or a similar curriculum on its own timeline.

This rolling-enrollment, multi-batch structure is driven by **market demand realities**: aspirants appear for UPSC at different points in their preparation journey, some preparing for the first time, others re-attempting after previous failures. Institutes accommodate this by launching new batches every 4-8 weeks throughout the year, ensuring that an aspirant who decides to join in June is not forced to wait until January to begin classes. While this model maximizes enrollment and revenue, it creates a **layered and operationally complex environment** that poses significant management challenges.

The operational challenges in this environment can be categorized into four dimensions:

**1. Faculty Sharing and Load Distribution:** The same faculty member is typically assigned to teach the same subject across multiple concurrent batches. This places heavy demands on the faculty member's time and cognitive bandwidth, leaving less time for preparation, doubt-clearing sessions, and individual student interaction.

**2. Batch Size Variability:** Actual enrollment frequently deviates from planned capacity — sometimes due to high demand (over-enrollment), sometimes due to dropouts. Both scenarios introduce **batch pressure** as a critical operational variable.

**3. Scheduling Complexity:** Coordinating timetables across 8-14 concurrent batches, each at a different syllabus stage, while sharing a limited pool of faculty across multiple subjects, is a combinatorially complex scheduling problem.

**4. Resource Allocation Under Concurrent Demand:** Resources such as classroom infrastructure, test-series evaluation teams, and doubt-clearing slots must be dynamically allocated across multiple simultaneously running batches.

The central question this study addresses is: **how do faculty utilization and batch pressure, individually and in combination, influence course completion outcomes in multi-batch UPSC coaching institutes?**

## 1.3 Problem Statement

The rapid growth of the UPSC coaching industry has outpaced the development of systematic operational management practices. Across the 26 batches studied in this research, **23 out of 26 batches (88.5%) experienced delays**, with an average delay of **9.08 days** and a maximum delay of **18 days**. Only 3 batches were completed on time.

This study proposes that the root causes of course completion delays are operational and managerial in nature — specifically linked to how faculty are utilized and how batch sizes are managed, and critically, the **interaction between these two variables**.

---

## 1.4 Research Objectives

- **RO1:** To examine the nature and strength of the relationship between faculty utilization (faculty load score) and course completion delay.
- **RO2:** To analyze the effect of batch size and batch pressure on course completion delay.
- **RO3:** To investigate the interaction effect between faculty load score and batch pressure on course completion delay.
- **RO4:** To compare course completion outcomes across Insights IAS, IAS Baba, and La Excellence.
- **RO5:** To derive evidence-based operational recommendations for UPSC coaching institute managers.

---

## 1.5 Research Questions

- **RQ1:** What is the nature and strength of the relationship between faculty load score and course completion delay?
- **RQ2:** How does batch pressure influence course completion delay, and are there identifiable threshold effects?
- **RQ3:** Does the interaction between batch pressure and faculty load score significantly predict course completion delay beyond their individual effects?
- **RQ4:** Are there statistically significant differences in course completion delays across the three institutes?

---

## 1.6 Scope of the Study

The study covers **26 batches** from three institutes — Insights IAS, IAS Baba, and La Excellence — spanning January 2024 to February 2025. The unit of analysis is the **batch**. The study focuses on operational input variables and does not examine student academic performance outcomes or teaching quality in a pedagogical sense.

---

## 1.7 Significance of the Study

This study makes original contributions at four levels:
- **Academic:** First empirical study applying operations management frameworks to UPSC coaching institute management
- **Practical:** Provides evidence-based decision framework for faculty deployment and batch size management
- **Student/Aspirant:** Creates basis for consumer-facing accountability standards
- **Policy:** Informs potential regulatory standards for the coaching sector
