# Chapter 6: Conclusions

## 6.1 Achievement of Research Objectives

All five research objectives have been fully achieved:

- RO1: Faculty Load Score established as dominant predictor (r = 0.847, p < 0.0001)
- RO2: Batch Pressure confirmed as significant predictor with threshold at BP = 1.05
- RO3: Interaction effect confirmed (beta = -0.1124, p = 0.0081, total R2 = 0.9094)
- RO4: Significant inter-institute differences confirmed (ANOVA F = 4.23, p = 0.027)
- RO5: Six evidence-based operational recommendations derived

## 6.2 Principal Conclusions

Conclusion 1: Course completion delay is systemic — 88.5% of batches delayed, average 9.08 days.

Conclusion 2: Faculty Load Score is the primary structural driver of delay (R2 contribution = 71.7%).

Conclusion 3: Batch Pressure amplifies delay risk with critical threshold at BP = 1.05.

Conclusion 4: Operational risk is multiplicative — worst case (High FLS + High BP) produces 15.40 days average delay vs. 4.92 days in best case.

Conclusion 5: La Excellence's lower Faculty Load Score strategy (FLS = 122.86) produces superior outcomes (mean delay 6.57 days) despite moderate-to-high batch pressure.

Conclusion 6: The UPSC coaching sector urgently needs evidence-based operational management frameworks.

## 6.3 Theoretical Implications

1. Confirms Johnston and Clark (2008) — service systems near capacity exhibit systematic quality degradation
2. Batch Pressure as normalized ratio is methodologically superior to absolute class size for multi-institutional comparisons
3. Interaction effects between supply-side (FLS) and demand-side (BP) variables are significant in educational operations

## 6.4 Practical Implications

- For managers: Use ORS = BP x FLS to monitor and manage combined risk monthly
- For faculty: Lower concurrent batch assignments and weekly hours are necessary for sustainable quality
- For students: Seek FLS and BP data before enrolling; prefer institutes with FLS less than 150 and BP less than 1.10
- For regulators: Empirical basis now exists for minimum operational standards in coaching institutes

## 6.5 Limitations

1. Sample size of 26 batches below standard regression recommendation
2. Three-institute purposive sample limits generalizability
3. Cross-sectional design limits causal inference
4. Self-reported teaching hours may have minor inaccuracies
5. Only course completion delay examined — student outcomes not linked

## 6.6 Directions for Future Research

1. Longitudinal study of FLS policy changes and their effects on delays
2. Link batch operational data to student examination performance
3. Apply framework to online/hybrid coaching platforms
4. Comparative study across IIT-JEE, NEET, banking examination coaching sectors
5. Intervention study implementing the six recommendations and tracking outcomes

## 6.7 Final Conclusion

Faculty Load Score and Batch Pressure are the primary operational drivers of course completion delay in multi-batch UPSC coaching institutes, with their interaction creating multiplicative risk. The Operational Risk Score (ORS = BP x FLS) framework provides a practical, evidence-based tool for proactive operational management.

The path forward is clear: manage Faculty Load Scores, control Batch Pressure, monitor their interaction, and benchmark against the best operational models. The evidence is available. The decision to act rests with institute managers — and ultimately, with the aspirants who hold these institutions accountable through their enrollment choices.
